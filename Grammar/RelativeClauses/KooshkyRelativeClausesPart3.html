1. "Of Which" (Formal Possession)

While "whose" is acceptable for inanimate objects in daily speech, "of which" is the preferred choice in high-level academic writing to show possession for things (non-humans).

The Formula

You are essentially breaking the possession apart. Instead of [Possessor + Possession], you create [Possession + of which].

[Noun], the [Part/Feature] of which [Verb/Clause]…

Essential Examples (Academic Contexts)

The theory: The theory, the limitations of which were discussed in the lecture, is outdated.
The research: The research paper, the methodology of which was criticized, will be revised.
The company: The company, the revenue of which has declined, is restructuring.

Three Rules for Usage:

1. "Of Which" is NOT for people.

Never use "of which" for people. Keep using "whose" for humans.

✓ Correct: The professor whose research is famous…
✗ Incorrect: The professor, the research of which is famous…

2. Always use commas (Non-defining).

Because "of which" adds descriptive, high-level information rather than essential identification, it is almost always set off by commas. Treat it as an "extra" detail.

3. Watch the word order.

The most natural (and safe) word order is: [Noun] + the + [feature] + of which…

Safe: The study, the results of which are controversial…
Avoid (Clunky): The study, of which the results are controversial… (Grammatically okay, but reads very stiffly and is harder to follow).
TOEFL Strategy: The "One-and-Done" Rule

Do not use "of which" in every sentence—it will make your essay sound like a legal document.

  • The Goal: Use it only once in an essay to show you can handle complex, formal structures.
  • The Benefit: When the grader sees a sentence like "The proposed policy, the implications of which are far-reaching, deserves closer scrutiny," they immediately recognize your high level of grammatical control. It is a very effective "marker" of an advanced writer.

2. Comma Rule Nuance (Meaning Changes)

Adding a comma doesn't just change the punctuation; it changes the logical meaning of your sentence. To decide whether to use a comma, ask yourself: Is this information necessary to identify exactly who or what I am talking about?

No Commas = The "Filter" (Essential Info)

When you write without commas, you are acting as a filter. You are narrowing a large group down to a specific sub-group. Without this info, the reader wouldn't know which one you mean.

Example: "The students who study hard usually get high scores."
The Logic: I am not talking about all students. I am filtering out the ones who don't study. I am only talking about the studious ones. The clause "who study hard" is the essential identity of the students I am discussing. If I remove it, the sentence becomes too vague ("The students usually get high scores"—which ones?).

With Commas = The "Comment" (Extra Info)

When you write with commas, the information is just a side note or an extra comment. You already know exactly who or what I'm talking about, so the clause isn't needed to identify them.

Example: "The students, who study hard, usually get high scores."
The Logic: Imagine I have a specific group of students sitting in front of me. I already know who they are. I am just adding a side comment: "By the way, they happen to study hard." If I deleted the clause, the sentence still makes perfect sense and you still know exactly who I am talking about.

The "Unique Noun" Rule

If the noun is unique, proper (names), or already specific, you must use commas because you don't need to "filter" it. The information is automatically extra.

Example (People): "My brother, who lives in Canada, is a doctor."
Why commas? You only have one specific brother you are talking about (or the listener knows exactly which one). You don't need the clause to identify him. You are just telling me a fact about him. (Compare: "My brother who lives in Canada is a doctor" suggests you have multiple brothers, and the clause is identifying which specific brother you mean.)
Example (Places): "Paris, which is the capital of France, is beautiful."
Why commas? Paris is already a unique, named city. You don't need the clause to know which Paris you are talking about. It is an extra detail.

The "Removal Test" (A Quick Checklist)

If you are unsure whether to use a comma, try deleting the relative clause from the sentence:

  1. If the sentence still makes perfect sense and refers to the exact same person or thing, the info is "extra." Use commas.
  2. If the sentence loses its meaning or becomes vague because you no longer know who or what the subject is, the info is "essential." Do not use commas.

Meaning Changes with the Same Words

One important thing to understand is that sometimes the words of the sentence stay exactly the same, but the meaning changes completely depending on the commas. The punctuation tells the reader how to interpret the information.

"My brothers who live in Canada are doctors."
Meaning: I have multiple brothers, but only the ones who live in Canada are doctors. The clause is identifying which brothers I am talking about.
"My brothers, who live in Canada, are doctors."
Meaning: All of my brothers live in Canada, and they are all doctors. The clause is just extra information about them.
The only difference is the commas, but the interpretation changes.
"The cars which are electric are quiet."
Meaning: Only the electric cars are quiet. Other cars may be noisy.
Equivalent to: Electric cars are generally quiet.
"The cars, which are electric, are quiet."
Meaning: All the cars are electric, and they are quiet. The clause is simply additional information about them.
Equivalent to: These cars are electric and also quiet.

These examples show that commas in relative clauses are not just punctuation—they control the logic of the sentence and determine whether the clause is identifying something specific or simply adding extra information.

TOEFL Tip:

Examiners look closely at comma usage because it proves you understand how to control the focus of your sentences. If you are writing about a specific, named entity (e.g., "The United Nations" or "My professor"), you should almost always use commas when adding extra details about it, because the subject is already uniquely identified.

3. Stacked (Nested) Relative Clauses

A stacked (or nested) relative clause happens when one relative clause appears inside another relative clause. In other words, you begin describing a noun, and inside that description you add another description about something within it.

This allows you to compress several related ideas into one sentence, which is common in academic writing where information is tightly connected.

Example:

Sentence: "The book that the professor who won the award wrote is famous."
To understand the structure, separate the ideas:
  1. The professor won the award.
  2. The professor wrote the book.
  3. The book is famous.
Now look at how they combine:
  • Main noun: the book
  • First relative clause: that the professor wrote
  • Inside that clause, we further describe the professor:
  • Second relative clause: who won the award
So the structure becomes:
The book
→ that the professor (who won the award) wrote
→ is famous.
The second clause is "nested" inside the first one.

Another example:

Sentence: "The policy that the committee that the university created proposed was controversial."
Underlying ideas:
  1. The university created a committee.
  2. The committee proposed a policy.
  3. The policy was controversial.
Combined sentence:
"The policy that the committee that the university created proposed was controversial."
Here again, the clause describing the committee sits inside the clause describing the policy.

Why This Structure Exists

Stacked relative clauses appear when information has a chain of relationships. For example:

  • person → wrote → book
  • committee → proposed → policy
  • scientist → conducted → experiment

If one of those elements also needs explanation, you insert another relative clause inside the first one.

Example: "The scientist who conducted the experiment that changed the theory became famous."
Ideas behind the sentence:
  1. An experiment changed a theory.
  2. A scientist conducted that experiment.
  3. The scientist became famous.
TOEFL Usage Strategy

This structure shows that you can handle complex logical relationships, which is valuable in academic writing. However, it can become difficult to read if overused.

Guidelines for safe use:
  • Limit yourself to two levels of clauses. More than that becomes confusing.
  • Use it when the information is logically connected in a chain.
  • Keep the sentence relatively short so the reader can follow the structure.
  • Do not use them to add too much information in each clause because it will become unreadable very fast.
Good academic-style example:
"The study that the researcher who specializes in climate models conducted received international attention."
Underlying ideas:
  1. The researcher specializes in climate models.
  2. The researcher conducted the study.
  3. The study received attention.

In this way, stacked relative clauses allow you to present several connected facts in one compact subject, which is why they sometimes appear in advanced academic English.

4. Clauses after Quantifiers (All / Some / Many)

In academic writing, relative clauses frequently appear after quantifiers—words that describe how much or how many of something we are talking about. These include words like all, some, many, most, few, several, anyone, everyone, and those.

The relative clause is used to define exactly which members of the group we mean. Because it identifies the group, the clause is usually essential, which means no commas are used.

Example:

Sentence: "All students who attend class regularly tend to perform better."
Meaning: Not all students perform better—only the subgroup that attends class regularly. The clause who attend class regularly defines which students we mean.
If you removed the clause, the meaning would change:
"All students tend to perform better."
This is a completely different claim, so the clause is clearly essential.

Another example:

Sentence: "Many of the books that I read this semester focus on environmental policy."
Meaning: I read many books, but the sentence refers specifically to the books that focus on environmental policy. The clause identifies the relevant subset of books.
Notice the structure here:
"many of the + noun + relative clause"
This pattern is extremely common in academic writing.
Example: "Some of the solutions that researchers proposed were impractical."
Ideas behind the sentence:
  1. Researchers proposed several solutions.
  2. Some of those solutions were impractical.
The relative clause that researchers proposed tells the reader exactly which solutions we are discussing.

Quantifiers with indefinite pronouns also commonly take relative clauses:

"Anyone who wants to participate must register."
Meaning: Any person who wants to participate is required to register.
"Everyone who attended the lecture received the materials."
Meaning: Only the people who attended the lecture received them.
These clauses are essential because they limit the meaning of broad words like anyone or everyone.

Another common TOEFL pattern is with those / the ones:

"Those who support the proposal argue that it will reduce costs."
Meaning: The people who support the proposal argue this point.
Here the clause who support the proposal identifies the group being discussed.
Usage Strategy for TOEFL Writing

Relative clauses after quantifiers are very useful because they help you make precise claims about groups, which is exactly what academic essays require.

For example:
Weak:
"Many students struggle in online courses."
Stronger:
"Many students who lack strong time‑management skills struggle in online courses."

The second sentence is better because the relative clause explains which students you are referring to. This kind of precision makes arguments clearer and more persuasive in TOEFL essays.

Key idea: quantifier + relative clause is a simple but powerful way to define a subgroup inside a larger population, which is a core skill in academic reasoning.

10. Non‑finite "To‑Infinitive" Relatives

A to‑infinitive relative is a shortened form of a relative clause that uses to + verb instead of "who/that/which + verb." It is called non‑finite because the verb does not show tense or agreement. This structure helps you express ideas in a shorter and more elegant way, which is common in academic English.

Standard form:
"She was the first student who finished the exam."

Reduced form:
"She was the first student to finish the exam."

Both sentences mean the same thing, but the second one is cleaner and more compact.

Understanding the Logic

Think of the infinitive phrase as replacing a relative clause that describes what someone does, can do, or should do.

Example:
"This is the best method that we can use."
Reduced version:
"This is the best method to use."
The phrase to use replaces the longer clause that we can use.
Another example:
"There is nothing that we need to do."
Reduced version:
"There is nothing to do."
Again, the infinitive replaces the relative clause.

Common Situations Where This Structure Appears

1. After words showing rank or order

This is one of the most common uses.

Examples:
"She was the first student to finish the exam."
"He was the last person to leave the building."
"This is the second experiment to demonstrate the effect."
Underlying idea:
"the first student who finished" → "the first student to finish"

2. After superlatives (best, easiest, most important)

Example:
"This is the best strategy to follow."
"This is the easiest way to solve the problem."
These structures frequently appear in academic explanations.

3. After words like something, nothing, anything

Examples:
"There is nothing to worry about."
"Do you have anything to add?"
"She found something to improve in the design."
Underlying idea:
"nothing that we should worry about" → "nothing to worry about"

Important Usage Note

The logical subject of the action is usually the noun before the infinitive.

Example:
"She was the first student to finish the exam."
Meaning: The student finished the exam.
Sometimes the subject is understood from context:
"This is the best method to use."
Meaning: The best method for us to use.
TOEFL Strategy

To‑infinitive relatives are extremely useful because they make sentences shorter and more natural, which improves clarity in timed writing.

Wordy:
"This is the most effective solution that researchers can implement."
More natural:
"This is the most effective solution to implement."

Using this structure occasionally in your essay shows that you can compress information efficiently, which is a hallmark of advanced academic writing.
