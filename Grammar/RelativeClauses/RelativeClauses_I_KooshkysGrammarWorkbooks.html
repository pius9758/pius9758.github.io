    Exercise 1 · Track the Relative Word

    Learn to identify what each relative word refers to and what job it performs inside its relative clause.

    How to Answer

    Question 1

    What does the relative word refer to?

    Usually, it refers to a noun or noun phrase in the independent clause. Sometimes, which refers to the whole idea before it.

    Question 2

    What is its role inside the relative clause?

    Do not only name the relative word. Explain its job: subject, object, object of a preposition, possession, place, time, complement, or whole-clause reference.

    • Find the relative word: who, whom, which, that, where, when, whose.
    • Find the noun, noun phrase, or whole idea it points back to.
    • Read only the relative clause and ask: what job is the relative word doing here?

    Role Bank

    Subject

    The relative word does the action in the relative clause.

    the student who asked the question

    Object

    The relative word receives the action in the relative clause.

    the article that we read

    Object of Preposition

    The relative word comes after a preposition in meaning.

    the advisor to whom I sent my plan

    Possessive Determiner

    Whose shows ownership before a noun.

    the student whose laptop stopped working

    Place / Time Adverbial

    Where shows place. When shows time.

    the room where we met · the day when classes started

    Complement

    The relative word completes the meaning after a verb such as be.

    the solution that we expected it to be

    Model Examples

    Model 1

    The professor who explained the assignment gave us helpful advice.

    Referent
    who refers to the professor.

    Role
    who is the subject of “who explained the assignment.”

    Model 2

    The article that we read for class was difficult but useful.

    Referent
    that refers to the article.

    Role
    that is the object of “we read.”

    Model 3

    The university extended the deadline, which reduced students’ stress.

    Referent
    which refers to the whole idea: the university extended the deadline.

    Role
    which is the subject of “which reduced students’ stress.”

    Model 4

    The workshop was not the practical training that I expected it to be.

    Referent
    that refers to the practical training.

    Role
    that is a complement in “I expected it to be that.”

    Exercise 2 · Connect Ideas Using Relative Clauses

    Combine related ideas into one smoother sentence. The goal is not to make every sentence longer; the goal is to connect information when it naturally belongs together.

    How to Connect Ideas

    Choose the shared noun or idea

    Find the person, thing, place, time, or whole situation that appears in both ideas. That shared point becomes the place where the relative clause attaches.

    Do not combine everything

    A good relative clause makes the sentence smoother. If the sentence becomes crowded or confusing, use two sentences instead.

    • Use who for people doing the action: students who commute.
    • Use that/which for things, ideas, assignments, policies, courses, and documents.
    • Use where for places and when for times.
    • Use whose to connect possession: a student whose laptop broke.
    • Use natural preposition patterns such as the course I registered for, and formal patterns such as the course for which I registered.
    • Use comma + which when the second idea comments on the whole first situation.

    Model Connections

    Model 1 · Identifying a group

    Many students prefer evening classes.

    These students work during the day.

    Connected sentence
    Many students who work during the day prefer evening classes.

    The relative clause identifies which students we are talking about.

    Model 2 · Showing a result

    The professor extended the deadline.

    This reduced students’ stress.

    Connected sentence
    The professor extended the deadline, which reduced students’ stress.

    Which refers to the whole first situation, not just the deadline.

    Practice C · Prepositions and Relative Clauses

    Combine the ideas in two ways when possible: a natural version with the preposition near the end, and a formal version with preposition + which/whom. Do not use preposition + that.

    Model 1 · Thing + preposition

    The theory is weak.

    The argument is based on the theory.

    Natural
    The theory that/which the argument is based on is weak.

    Formal
    The theory on which the argument is based is weak.

    The formal version moves on before which. Never write on that.

    Model 2 · Person + preposition

    The advisor was helpful.

    I spoke to the advisor yesterday.

    Natural
    The advisor I spoke to yesterday was helpful.

    Formal
    The advisor to whom I spoke yesterday was helpful.

    The natural version omits the object relative word. The formal version uses to whom because the noun is a person.

    Exercise 3 · Build a Sentence

    Build accurate, natural sentences with relative clauses. The tasks become gradually harder: chunks, words, anchored blanks, and word-level banks with hidden extra words.

    How to Build

    Use the noun as your base

    First find the noun being described. Place the relative clause immediately after that noun: the article that we read, not the article was useful that we read.

    Avoid repeated pronouns

    If the relative word already acts as the object, do not repeat it with it, him, her, or they. Write the article that we read, not the article that we read it.

    • In Practice A and B, use all the chunks or words.
    • In Practice C, each blank is large because more than one word may go into it.
    • In Practice D, each bank is word-level and contains one extra word that does not belong. Leave it out if it wrongly repeats the relative word.
    • Add commas only when the relative clause gives extra information or when which comments on the whole previous idea.

    Model Builds

    Model 1 · Jumbled Chunks

    Bank
    answered the question which helped the discussion continue The student who had read the article carefully

    Answer
    The student who had read the article carefully answered the question, which helped the discussion continue.

    The first relative clause describes the student. The final which refers to the whole action: the student answered the question.

    Model 2 · Trap Word

    Exercise 4 · Essential or Extra?

    Use commas to show meaning. A relative clause can identify exactly which person or thing you mean, or it can add extra information about someone or something already clear.

    How to Decide

    Essential information: no commas

    If the relative clause identifies the noun, do not put commas around it. The clause answers: Which one? or Which people?

    Extra information: use commas

    If the noun is already clear, use commas. The relative clause adds useful information, but the sentence still identifies the noun without it.

    • Do not use that after a comma. Use who for people or which for things and ideas.
    • Use comma + which when which comments on the whole previous idea.
    • Ask yourself: “Without this clause, would the reader still know exactly who or what I mean?”

    Models

    Model 1 · Essential

    Students who miss the deadline must contact the department office.

    No commas. The clause who miss the deadline identifies which students. Not all students must contact the office.

    Model 2 · Extra

    My academic advisor, who helped me choose my courses, also explained the graduation requirements.

    Use commas. My academic advisor is already specific. The relative clause adds extra information about that advisor.

    Exercise 5 · Relative Clause Compression Challenge

    Combine short, choppy ideas into smoother sentences. Your goal is not to make the longest sentence possible. Your goal is to make the clearest sentence possible.

    The Goal: Smooth, Not Heavy

    Good compression

    A good relative clause removes repetition and adds useful information near the noun it describes.

    The workshop that I attended last week helped me organize my essays more clearly.

    Bad compression

    A bad relative clause hides the main idea inside a sentence that becomes too long or hard to follow.

    The workshop that I attended last week that the department organized that many students joined helped me.

    • Use a relative clause when it removes repetition or makes the sentence flow better.
    • Do not put every idea into one sentence just because you can.
    • If a sentence has too many attached clauses, split it into two clear sentences.
    • Keep the main idea easy to find.

    This is good compression. The relative clause identifies and describes the article without making the sentence heavy.

    Do not force all four ideas into one sentence. One relative clause is useful; the remaining information is clearer in a second sentence.

    Exercise 6 · Sentential Which for Flow

    Use which after a complete idea to show a result, effect, reaction, or evaluation. The goal is not to use which everywhere. The goal is to make TOEFL responses smoother when two short sentences are closely connected.

    How Sentential Which Works

    It refers to a whole idea

    In The professor extended the deadline, which reduced students’ stress, which does not mean deadline. It means the whole action: the professor extended the deadline.

    It needs a comma

    Use a comma before this kind of which. It is extra information about the result or effect of the previous idea.

    It should add real value

    A good which clause explains a result: which made the task easier, which caused confusion, which helped students prepare.

    It can become vague

    If the previous sentence has too many ideas, which may be unclear. In that case, use a new sentence with this, this change, or this decision.

    Models

    Model 1 · Smooth Result

    Before
    The university added more buses. This made commuting easier for students.

    After

    The which clause explains the result of the university’s action.

    Model 2 · Better as Two Sentences

    Too Heavy

    Better

    The original sentence has too many ideas before which. A new sentence with this is clearer.

    Some students try to make their writing sound advanced by adding relative clauses everywhere. This often creates repetition, wrong pronouns, wrong commas, or sentences that are too heavy.     A sentence is not better just because it is longer. If the relative clause creates repetition, comma problems, or confusion, remove or rebuild it.

    Exercise 12 · Full-Response Error Repair

    Now you will repair relative-clause problems inside complete TOEFL-style responses. Your goal is not only to fix grammar. Your goal is to make the whole response clearer, smoother, and more natural.

    What You Are Practicing

    Find the real problem

    Do not correct words randomly. Ask what the relative word refers to and what job it has inside the relative clause.

    Fix different error types

    You will repair repeated pronouns, wrong relative words, missing prepositions, comma problems, unclear references, and overused which chains.

    Protect correct structures

    Some relative clauses are already correct. A good editor does not damage a good sentence just because it looks complex.

    Keep the TOEFL task natural

    An email should still sound polite. A discussion post should still sound clear. A speaking answer should still be easy to say aloud.

    Repair Method

    1. Find the relative word: who, whom, which, that, where, when, or whose.
    2. Ask what it points to: a person, thing, place, time, possession, or the whole previous idea.
    3. Check its job: subject, object, object of preposition, place/time adverbial, possession, complement, or whole-clause reference.
    4. Look for common traps: who he, which it, where is located, students whose they, the day which, to who, and that after a comma.
    5. Rewrite for clarity: if a tiny edit leaves the sentence awkward, rebuild the sentence.

    Models

    Model 1 · Repeated pronoun + wrong place word

    Problem version

    Better version
    The article that we read was useful, but the website that lists the sources was difficult to use.

    Why: In that we read, that is the object, so do not repeat it with it. In the website that lists..., the relative word is the subject of lists, so where is not possible.

    Model 2 · Correct structure that should stay

    Problem version
    The professor extended the deadline, which helped many students finish their essays.

    Better version
    No correction needed.

    Why: Here which refers to the whole idea that the professor extended the deadline. This is a strong and natural structure. Do not replace every which automatically.

    Exercise 13 · Upgrade Weak TOEFL Texts

    In this exercise, you will take clear but simple TOEFL-style answers and make them smoother by adding useful relative clauses. Do not add relative clauses just to look advanced. Add them when they reduce repetition, connect ideas, or make a noun more specific.

    What Makes a Good Upgrade?

    Less repetition

    Good relative clauses remove repeated nouns: students who work part-time is smoother than students work part-time. These students...

    Clear reference

    The reader should know exactly what who, which, that, where, when, or whose refers to.

    Natural length

    A sentence with one useful relative clause is often better than a sentence with three heavy relative clauses.

    TOEFL style

    Email answers should stay polite, Academic Discussion answers should stay focused, and speaking answers should still be easy to say aloud.

    • Use relative clauses to connect information that belongs together.
    • Keep separate sentences when combining would make the idea heavy or confusing.
    • Avoid repeated object pronouns such as that I need it or which students use them.
    • Use sentential which only when it clearly refers to the whole previous idea.

    Exercise 14 · Use Relative Clauses in Your Own TOEFL Practice

    Use relative clauses in real TOEFL writing and speaking practice. The goal is not to count grammar structures. The goal is to make your ideas clearer, smoother, and more precise.

    Purpose

    Use many, but use them naturally

    Try to use as many relative clauses as you can without damaging clarity. A good relative clause should reduce repetition, identify a noun, add useful detail, or connect a result smoothly.

    Remove forced clauses

    If a relative clause only makes the sentence longer, remove it. Strong TOEFL grammar is controlled, not crowded.

    Practice A · Writing Response Challenge

    Choose one TOEFL-style writing prompt and write a complete response. After writing, revise your answer and add useful relative clauses where they genuinely improve the response.

    1

    Email: Write to a professor about a problem with a class activity, an assignment, a schedule, or a campus service.

    2

    Academic Discussion: Should universities require students to take some courses outside their major?

    3

    Academic Discussion: Should schools allow students to choose more of their own assignments?

    • Use at least one relative clause that identifies a person, group, object, class, idea, or place.
    • Use one relative clause only if it clearly improves flow or avoids repetition.
    • Try to include sentential which once if it naturally shows a result.
    • Do not use long chains of relative clauses in the same sentence.
    Self-Check · Practice A
    Underline every relative clause you used. For each one, ask: Does it identify something, add useful information, show possession/place/time, or connect a result?
    Check for repeated-pronoun errors: that I read it, who he explained, whose her laptop, which they are not specialists.
    Check comma meaning: no comma for identifying information; commas for extra information and sentential which.
