Relative Clauses: The Secret to High-Scoring TOEFL Writing

Relative clauses are the "glue" of English sentences. They allow you to combine simple, choppy sentences into complex, sophisticated thoughts. Using them shows the examiner that you can control sentence structure: a key requirement for higher scores.

Introduction: What is a Relative Clause?

Imagine you want to tell your advisor about a book. You could say:

"The book is very interesting. I read the book yesterday."

That sounds repetitive. Instead, use a relative clause to glue them together:

"The book that I read yesterday is very interesting."

Quick Definitions:

  • Phrase: A group of words that does not have both a subject and a complete verb. A phrase can add information, but it is not a complete sentence by itself. Example: "in the library".
  • Clause: A group of words with a subject and a verb. Example: "the student studied". Some clauses are complete sentences, but some are not.
  • Independent clause: A clause that can stand alone as a full sentence. Example: "The book is interesting."
  • Dependent clause: A clause that has a subject and a verb, but cannot stand alone as a full sentence. It depends on another clause. Example: "that I read yesterday".
  • Relative clause: A dependent clause that describes a noun. In "the book that I read yesterday", the relative clause is "that I read yesterday", and it describes "the book".

Before the Rules: Main Clause vs. Relative Clause

A relative clause is not usually the main sentence. It is a smaller clause inside a bigger sentence.

The main clause gives the central message of the sentence. It can usually stand alone.

The relative clause gives extra or identifying information about a noun. It usually cannot stand alone.

Example:
"The book that I read yesterday is very interesting."

Main clause: "The book is very interesting."

This can stand alone as a complete sentence.

Relative clause: "that I read yesterday"

This cannot stand alone. If you say only "that I read yesterday", the listener will ask: "What did you read?"

The relative clause normally comes right after the noun it describes:

Pattern:
noun + relative clause + rest of the sentence

"The student who answered the question received extra credit."
Noun = student
Relative clause = who answered the question
Rest of the sentence = received extra credit

Important: Do not confuse the subject of the whole sentence with the subject inside the relative clause.

In "The student who answered the question received extra credit," the subject of the whole sentence is "The student who answered the question". The verb of the whole sentence is "received".

Inside the relative clause, "who" is the subject of "answered".

The Relative Word Does Two Jobs

Words like who, whom, which, that, where, when, whose are not only "connectors." They connect the clause to a noun, but they also have a grammar job inside the relative clause.

Think of the relative word as a bridge and a replacement.

It is a bridge because it connects the relative clause to the noun before it.

It is a replacement because it replaces the repeated noun inside the smaller clause.

How a Relative Clause Is Built

Start with two simple sentences:

"I read the article."
"The article explains the problem."

The repeated noun is "the article." If we want the first sentence to describe "the article," we replace the repeated noun with a relative word:

"The article that I read explains the problem."

Here, "that I read" describes "the article." Inside the relative clause, "that" is the object of the verb "read". It answers the question "I read what?"

The Relative Word Can Have Different Jobs

The same relative word can sometimes have different jobs. For example, which can be the subject of the relative clause, the object of the verb, or the object of a preposition.

Subject

Example: The professor who teaches biology is popular.

Who does the action teaches. It is the subject of the relative clause.

Object of a verb

Example: The article that I read was useful.

That receives the action read. It means “I read the article.”

Object of a preposition

Example: The advisor whom I spoke to was helpful.

Whom is connected to the preposition to. It means “I spoke to the advisor.”

Place adverb

Example: The lab where we did the experiment was small.

Where means in that place. It replaces in the lab.

Time adverb

Example: I remember the day when the course began.

When means on that day or at that time.

Reason adverb

Example: The reason why attendance improved was simple.

Why means for that reason. It usually follows the noun reason.

Possession

Example: The student whose essay won the prize is in my class.

Whose shows possession. It means “the student’s essay.”

Whole-situation subject

Example: The class was cancelled, which surprised everyone.

Which is the subject of surprised and refers to the whole previous situation.

Why This Matters for TOEFL Writing

If you know the job of the relative word, you can make better grammar choices.

Rule 1: If the relative word is the subject, do not add another subject.

Correct: "The professor who teaches biology is popular."

Incorrect: "The professor who he teaches biology is popular."

Why? Because who already means the professor inside the relative clause. You do not need he.

Rule 2: If the relative word is the object, the relative clause still needs its own subject.

Correct: "The article that I read was useful."

Incorrect: "The article that read was useful."

Why? In this meaning, that is the object of read, so we still need the subject I.

Rule 3: If the relative word is the object of a preposition, the preposition must still be in the meaning.

Natural: "The advisor whom I spoke to was helpful."

More formal: "The advisor to whom I spoke was helpful."

Incorrect: "The advisor whom I spoke was helpful."

Why? You speak to someone. The preposition to is necessary.

A Special Note About "What"

What is different from normal relative words like who, which, and that. It usually means "the thing that". For this reason, it normally does not come right after a noun.

Incorrect after a noun:

"The thing what I need is more time."

Correct:

"The thing that I need is more time."

"What I need is more time."

In "What I need is more time," what means "the thing that." It is not just connecting to a noun before it; it already includes the noun idea inside itself.

TOEFL Tip: When you are describing a noun, use that, which, who, whom, where, when, or whose. Use what mainly when you mean "the thing that".

1. The Relative Pronouns

Think of these as bridges connecting your ideas. Remember: each one also has a job inside the dependent clause.

  • Who: For people, especially when the person is the subject of the relative clause. Example: "the student who answered".
  • Whom: For people when the person is the object of a verb or preposition. This is formal. Example: "the advisor whom I contacted".
  • Which: For things, animals, or ideas. It can be a subject or object inside the relative clause. Example: "the policy which changed the system" / "the policy which the school changed".
  • That: For people or things in essential information. It can be a subject or object. Example: "the course that requires a project" / "the course that I chose".
  • Where: For places. It usually means "in/at/on which place". Example: "the library where I study".
  • When: For time (day, year, moment, period, etc.). It usually means "at/on/in which time". Example: "the semester when I took biology".
  • Why: For reasons. It usually comes after "reason". Example: "the reason why students prefer online classes".
  • Whose: For possession. It works for both people and things. Example: "the student whose essay won" / "the book whose cover is blue".
  • What: Special case. It usually means "the thing that" and normally does not come directly after a noun. Example: "What I need is more time."

2. When to Use Which Relative Pronoun?

For People (Who vs. Whom)

  • Use Who when they are the ones doing the action.
Example: The professor who teaches History is very kind. (The professor is doing the teaching.)
  • Use Whom when they are the ones receiving the action. (This is formal and impressive in writing!)
Example: The student whom I met yesterday is from Italy. (I am the one doing the meeting; the student is receiving it.)

For Things/Ideas (Which vs. That)

Both which and that can refer to things, objects, animals, or abstract ideas. However, they are used slightly differently in formal writing.

Basic idea:

  • That is usually used for essential (defining) information.
  • Which is often used for extra (non-essential) information, especially in formal writing.

Think of it like this:

That = necessary information
Which = additional information

Example with that (essential information):

There are many yoga classes. You want to talk about a specific one.

Sentence:

"The yoga class that caused my back pain was very intense."

Meaning: the clause identifies which class you are talking about. Without it, the sentence becomes unclear.

Incorrect version:

"The yoga class was very intense."
(This does not explain which class you mean.)

Example with which (extra information):

"The yoga class, which was quite difficult, caused my back pain."

Meaning: we already know which yoga class we are talking about. The clause only adds extra description.

You could remove the clause and the sentence still makes sense:

"The yoga class caused my back pain."

Another comparison:

Essential information:

"The computer that I bought yesterday is already broken."

Extra information:

"My computer, which I bought yesterday, is already broken."

In the first sentence, the clause identifies which computer.

In the second sentence, we already know which computer it is (my computer), and the clause only adds extra detail.

Important TOEFL note:

In formal writing, that is normally NOT used after commas.

After a comma, we almost always use which.

Correct:
"The report, which I finished last night, is on your desk."

Incorrect:
"The report, that I finished last night, is on your desk."

For Places (Where)

Example: I visited the university where I want to study.

For Time (When)

Use when to refer to time nouns such as day, time, year, moment, period, or semester.

Structure:

time noun + when + clause

Example:
"I remember the day when I started learning English."
Here, the clause describes which day.
Another example:
"The moment when I felt the pain was during a difficult stretch."

Important note:

"When" usually replaces "at which" or "on which."

Example:
"I remember the day when we met."
= "I remember the day on which we met."

For TOEFL writing, when is clearer and more natural than at/on which.

TOEFL Tip:

Sometimes native speakers drop when and simply use that or nothing.

Example:
"The day when I hurt my back was yesterday."
"The day that I hurt my back was yesterday."
"The day I hurt my back was yesterday."

All three are correct, but using when is often clearer for learners.

For Reasons (Why)

Use why after the noun reason to explain a reason.

Structure:

the reason + why + clause

Example:
"The reason why many students choose online classes is flexibility."
Here, the clause explains the reason.

TOEFL Tip:

You can also say "the reason that many students choose online classes" or simply "the reason many students choose online classes." All are common, but why is clear for learners.

For Ownership (Whose)

Important: Use "whose" for possession. It works for both people and things.

  • Person Example: The student whose bag is on the floor is sitting there.
  • Object Example: The car whose engine is old is broken. (Even though a car isn't a person, "whose" describes the possession of the engine.)

3. Prepositions in Relative Clauses: Natural vs. Formal

Some relative clauses need a preposition because the verb, adjective, or noun inside the clause needs one. For example, we say speak to someone, depend on something, participate in something, and benefit from something. When you build a relative clause, you must keep that preposition somewhere in the sentence.

Key idea: The relative word may be the object of a preposition.

Simple sentence: I spoke to the advisor.

Relative clause: The advisor whom I spoke to was helpful.

The word whom refers to the advisor, but inside the relative clause it is connected to the preposition to.

Two Common Patterns

Natural pattern

noun + relative word + subject + verb + preposition

The preposition stays near the end of the relative clause.

Example: The advisor who/whom I spoke to was helpful.

More natural: The advisor I spoke to was helpful.

Formal pattern

noun + preposition + whom/which + subject + verb

The preposition moves before the relative word. This sounds more academic.

Example: The advisor to whom I spoke was helpful.

Important: After a preposition, do not use that.

Correct: The theory on which the argument is based is weak.

Incorrect: The theory on that the argument is based is weak.

For People

For people, the natural form often uses who, whom, that, or no relative word. The formal form uses preposition + whom.

Natural: The professor who I sent my draft to replied quickly.
Also natural: The professor I sent my draft to replied quickly.
Formal: The professor to whom I sent my draft replied quickly.
Natural: The students that I worked with were responsible.
Formal: The students with whom I worked were responsible.

For Things, Ideas, and Academic Nouns

For things and ideas, the natural form uses which, that, or no relative word. The formal form uses preposition + which. This formal pattern is especially useful with academic nouns such as method, theory, policy, system, process, issue, standard, and condition.

Natural: The theory that the article is based on is not convincing.
Formal: The theory on which the article is based is not convincing.
Natural: The platform that students submit assignments through is easy to use.
Formal: The platform through which students submit assignments is easy to use.
Natural: The conditions that students perform best under are not always available.
Formal: The conditions under which students perform best are not always available.
Natural: The support that many learners cannot succeed without is often expensive.
Formal: The support without which many learners cannot succeed is often expensive.

For Places: Where vs. Preposition + Which

Where is usually the clearest and most natural choice for places. A formal alternative is in/at/on + which, depending on the place meaning.

Natural: The library where I study is quiet.
Formal: The library in which I study is quiet.
Natural: The website where students register often crashes.
More precise/formal: The website through which students register often crashes.

TOEFL Tip: For real physical places, where is usually enough. For abstract situations, systems, and processes, in which or another precise preposition can sound more academic.

Example: Students need an environment in which they can ask questions freely.

For Time: When vs. Preposition + Which

When is natural for time nouns. Formal writing may use on which for days, in which for years or periods, and during which for periods of time.

Natural: I remember the day when the course began.
Formal: I remember the day on which the course began.
Natural: The semester when I took biology was challenging.
Formal: The semester during which I took biology was challenging.

For Reasons: Why vs. For Which

After the noun reason, why is natural. The formal version is for which.

Natural: The reason why many students avoid office hours is anxiety.
Formal: The reason for which many students avoid office hours is anxiety.

With Whose

Whose can also appear after a preposition, especially in formal writing. This is less common, but it is useful when the noun after whose belongs to a person or organization.

Natural: The professor whose class I joined was very clear.
More formal: The professor in whose class I learned this method was very clear.
Formal: The organization with whose support the project continued helped many students.

What About "What"?

What does not normally describe a noun before it. It already means the thing that. For this reason, do not write the topic what or the problem what.

Incorrect

The topic what the lecture focused on was difficult.

Correct

The topic that the lecture focused on was difficult.

Or: What the lecture focused on was difficult.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Incorrect: The advisor whom I spoke was helpful.
Correct: The advisor whom I spoke to was helpful.
Formal: The advisor to whom I spoke was helpful.
Incorrect: The university at that I studied is famous.
Correct: The university where I studied is famous.
Formal: The university at which I studied is famous.
Incorrect: The topic which we discussed about was important.
Correct: The topic that/which we discussed was important.
Why? We say discuss a topic, not discuss about a topic.

TOEFL Strategy: In speaking and emails, use the natural form most of the time: the professor I spoke to, the issue we talked about. In academic writing, use the formal form once or twice when it fits an academic idea: the method by which, the principle on which, the conditions under which. Do not force it into every sentence.

4. Special Skill: Using "Which" to Summarize a Situation

This is a high-level skill that TOEFL graders love. You can use "which" to describe the entire situation mentioned in the previous part of the sentence, not just one noun.

  • Standard Sentence: I missed the bus. This made me late.
  • Complex Sentence: I missed the bus, which made me late for class.

The "which" here refers to the entire event of missing the bus. It creates a smooth flow that sounds very native-like.

Punctuation:

When using "which" this way, you always have to use a comma before it.

5. How to Use Multiple Relative Clauses (Sentence Combining)

Once you are comfortable, you can combine ideas using more than one clause. But be careful: Do not make the sentence too long, or the meaning becomes unclear.

  • Simple: The professor is nice. He teaches History. He lives in London.
  • Combined: The professor, who teaches History and who lives in London, is very nice.

Self-Correction Rule: Only use two clauses if the information is important. If the sentence feels like it's becoming a "run-on" (too long), break it into two sentences instead.

6. The "Comma Rule" (Defining vs. Non-Defining)

Don't worry about the formal names; just remember this:

  • If the info is EXTRA (use commas):
Example: My roommate, who loves yoga, goes to the gym daily.
(I have only one roommate. The fact that she loves yoga is just extra info. I don't need it to identify her.)
  • If the info is ESSENTIAL (no commas):
Example: The student who sits in the front row is very smart.
(I am talking about a specific student—the one in the front row. Without this, you don't know which student I mean.)

7. Omitting the Relative Word (Advanced but Very Common)

In many relative clauses, the relative word (that, which, who, whom, when) can be omitted if it is the object of the clause, not the subject.

This makes sentences sound more natural and less formal.

Basic Pattern

Full relative clause:
Noun + relative word + subject + verb

Reduced version:
Noun + subject + verb

Example:

Full:
"The book that I bought yesterday is interesting."

Omitted:
"The book I bought yesterday is interesting."

Both are correct.

More Examples

Full:
"The student whom I met yesterday is from Italy."
Omitted:
"The student I met yesterday is from Italy."
Full:
"The movie that we watched last night was excellent."
Omitted:
"The movie we watched last night was excellent."
Full:
"The day when we met changed my life."
Omitted:
"The day we met changed my life."

Important Rule

You cannot omit the relative word if it is the subject of the clause.

Example:

Correct:
"The professor who teaches History is very kind."

Incorrect:
"The professor teaches History is very kind."

Why? Because "who" is the subject of the clause.

Think of it like this:

You can omit the relative word only if another subject remains in the clause.

Example:
"The book that I read"
Subject = I
So that can disappear.

But in:

"The book that was written by Tolstoy"
The relative word acts as the subject of the clause, so it cannot disappear.

TOEFL Writing Advice

For TOEFL writing:

  • Using the full relative clause (that / which / who) is always safe.
  • Omitting the word sounds more natural but slightly more informal.
  • Avoid overusing omission in academic essays if clarity becomes difficult.

Example (safe TOEFL style):
"The article that I read yesterday explains the issue clearly."

📝 Practice Checklist

Try to combine these pairs:

  1. The email is long. I received the email today. (Use "that" or "which")
  2. The advisor is helpful. I spoke to the advisor yesterday. (Use "whom")
  3. The lab is closed. We do experiments in the lab. (Use "where")
  4. The chair is broken. The chair's leg is loose. (Use "whose")
  5. The professor cancelled the exam. This surprised everyone. (Use "which" to summarize)
  6. I will never forget that day. We met for the first time then. (Use "when")

Answer Key:

  1. The email that/which I received today is long.
  2. The advisor whom I spoke to yesterday is helpful.
  3. The lab where we do experiments is closed.
  4. The chair whose leg is loose is broken.
  5. The professor cancelled the exam, which surprised everyone.
  6. I will never forget the day when we met for the first time.
