Relative Clauses: Part 2 – Advanced Subjects I

Congratulations on mastering the basics. Now that you have the foundation, it is time to look at the "Master Level" structures. These are used by native speakers and high-level academic writers to make sentences more compact, precise, and professional.

1. Preposition + Relative Pronoun (Formal Structure)

This structure, called fronting, is used in formal and academic writing. Instead of placing the preposition at the end of the clause, you move it before the relative pronoun.

Standard (neutral / spoken style)

  • The university that I studied at is famous.
  • The professor who I spoke to is helpful.
  • The theory which the argument is based on is weak.

Formal (academic style)

  • The university at which I studied is famous.
  • The professor to whom I spoke is helpful.
  • The theory on which the argument is based is weak.

Both versions are grammatically correct. The difference is formality and tone, not meaning. This structure is much more formal and can be used to show off your grammar skills!

When Should You Use It?

✅ Use it when:

  • You are writing formally.
  • The noun is abstract or academic (theory, method, system, issue, policy).
  • You want more variety in sentence structure.

Good academic examples:

  • The method by which data are collected must be reliable.
  • The system on which the company depends is outdated.
  • The principle on which the law is based is fairness.
  • The research to which the article refers was published last year.

These sound natural in formal writing.

⚠️ Do NOT use it too often

If you overuse it, your writing may sound unnatural. Especially if you use it in non-academic, conversational sentences:

  • The chair on which I sat was uncomfortable.
  • The friend with whom I went to the mall is kind.

These are correct, but they sound too formal for simple topics.

Use it mainly for:

  • ideas
  • institutions
  • research
  • formal topics

Important Grammar Rules

1️⃣ After a preposition, you cannot use "that."

Correct:

  • The university at which I studied
  • The person to whom I spoke

Incorrect:

  • The university at that I studied

2️⃣ Use "whom" for people, "which" for things.

People:

  • The student to whom I explained the problem
  • The manager for whom she works

Things:

  • The method by which they solved the issue
  • The city in which she was born

3️⃣ "Where" and "When" are often simpler alternatives

Instead of:

  • The university at which I studied

You can say:

  • The university where I studied

Instead of:

  • The year in which I graduated

You can say:

  • The year when I graduated

Both are correct.
"At which" and "in which" sound more formal.
"Where" and "when" sound more natural.

Does It Change the Meaning?

Usually, no. It changes the tone, not the meaning.

Compare:

  • The professor who I spoke to was helpful.
  • The professor to whom I spoke was helpful.

Same meaning. Different level of formality.

TOEFL Strategy

In a TOEFL essay:

✅ Use this structure once or twice to show range.

❌ Do not use it in every paragraph.

Strong example for TOEFL:

  • The theory on which this argument is based is not convincing.
  • The method by which researchers collect data must be reliable.

These sound academic, natural, and controlled.

2. Reduced Relative Clauses (Active)

A reduced relative clause shortens a sentence by removing the relative pronoun (who/that/which/etc.) and sometimes the verb "be."

When the verb describes an active action done by the noun, the verb usually becomes an -ing form (present participle).

Basic pattern:

Full clause:

noun + who/that/which + verb

Reduced clause:

noun + verb‑ing

Examples:

  • Standard: Students who study abroad gain independence.
  • Reduced: Students studying abroad gain independence.
  • Standard: The company that owns the factory is closing.
  • Reduced: The company owning the factory is closing.
  • Standard: People who live near the airport complain about noise.
  • Reduced: People living near the airport complain about noise.
  • Standard: Researchers who investigate climate change need reliable data.
  • Reduced: Researchers investigating climate change need reliable data.

Why this structure is used:

Reduced clauses make writing:

  • shorter
  • more efficient
  • more academic

Academic writing often prefers compact structures instead of long relative clauses.

Long:

Students who participate in internships often gain valuable experience.

Reduced:

Students participating in internships often gain valuable experience.

The second sentence feels tighter and more formal.

When reduction is possible:

You cannot reduce the clause when the relative pronoun is the object.

Example:

The student who I met yesterday is friendly.

Incorrect reduction:

The student meeting yesterday is friendly.

This does not work because "student" is not performing the action.

3. Reduced Relative Clauses (Passive)

Reduced relative clauses can also appear in passive structures. In this case, the clause originally contains:

which/that + be + past participle (verb 3)

When we reduce it, we remove the relative pronoun and the verb "be," leaving only the past participle.

Basic pattern:

Full clause:

noun + which/that + be + past participle

Reduced clause:

noun + past participle

Examples:

  • Standard: The article that was published last year is excellent.
  • Reduced: The article published last year is excellent.
  • Standard: All the documents that were submitted by the students were graded.
  • Reduced: All the documents submitted by the students were graded.
  • Standard: The book that was written by the professor became very popular.
  • Reduced: The book written by the professor became very popular.
  • Standard: The technology that was developed in Japan is very advanced.
  • Reduced: The technology developed in Japan is very advanced.

Why this works:

The past participle already contains the passive meaning, so the verb "be" becomes unnecessary.

Example:

the article which was published → the article published

The meaning remains passive.

Very common academic patterns:

Passive reduced clauses appear constantly in academic writing because they focus on results or objects, not the person performing the action.

Examples:

The study conducted in 2020 revealed important results.

The data collected during the experiment were carefully analyzed.

The system developed by engineers improved efficiency.

4. Reduced Relative Clauses ("Being")

Sometimes the original clause is passive and continuous, meaning the action is happening right now. These clauses use the structure:

is / are / was / were being + past participle

When reduced, the words which/that + is/are disappear, leaving:

being + past participle

Basic pattern:

Full clause:

noun + which/that + is/are being + past participle

Reduced clause:

noun + being + past participle

Examples:

  • Standard: The proposal that is being considered is very complex.
  • Reduced: The proposal being considered is very complex.
  • Standard: The students who are being taught by Dr. Smith are lucky.
  • Reduced: The students being taught by Dr. Smith are lucky.
  • Standard: The building that is being constructed downtown will be very tall.
  • Reduced: The building being constructed downtown will be very tall.
  • Standard: The experiment that is being conducted now may change the results.
  • Reduced: The experiment being conducted now may change the results.

Why "being" appears:

In passive continuous forms, "being" carries the progressive meaning (the action is ongoing).

Compare:

  • The article published last year (completed action)
  • The article being published this month (action in progress)

So "being" signals that the action is currently happening.

Examples:

The research being conducted at the university is groundbreaking.

The policy being discussed by the government may change soon.

The technology being developed by engineers could transform transportation.

Style note:

This structure is slightly formal and academic, so it should not be overused. Long chains of participle phrases can make sentences difficult to read.

Clear:

The study being conducted this year focuses on climate change.

Too heavy:

The study being conducted this year examining several environmental variables affecting global ecosystems…

Shorter sentences are usually better.

5. Free Relative Clauses (What / Whoever / Whatever)

A free relative clause (sometimes called a nominal relative clause) is unique because it does not modify a noun. Instead, the relative pronoun itself acts as the noun and contains the noun's meaning.

Basic Pattern

In standard relative clauses, you have a noun followed by a relative word:

  • The thing that you said was true.

In a free relative clause, the noun is merged into the pronoun:

  • What you said was true. (where what = the thing that)

The Three Main Free Relative Pronouns

1️⃣ What (meaning: "the thing that" or "the things that")

Used to refer to actions, ideas, or objects without naming them.

  • Standard: The thing that you did helped us a lot.
  • Free: What you did helped us a lot.
  • Standard: We must protect the things that remain.
  • Free: We must protect what remains.

2️⃣ Whoever (meaning: "any person who" or "the person who")

Used to refer to an unknown or unspecified person.

  • Standard: Any person who finishes the test first may leave.
  • Free: Whoever finishes the test first may leave.
  • Standard: I will support the person who is elected.
  • Free: I will support whoever is elected.

3️⃣ Whatever (meaning: "anything that" or "it does not matter what")

Used when the specific choice or thing does not matter.

  • Standard: You can choose anything that you want.
  • Free: You can choose whatever you want.
  • Standard: The choices that you make will shape your future.
  • Free: Whatever you choose will shape your future.

3.5 Whichever (meaning: "any one of a limited set")

Used when you have a specific, defined range of options to choose from (unlike "whatever", "whichever" is usually NOT open-ended).

  • Standard: Choose any one of the two books that you prefer.
  • Free: Choose whichever you prefer.
  • Standard: The candidate that wins the election—either A or B—will change the policy.
  • Free: Whichever candidate wins—A or B—will change the policy.

Comparison: "Whatever" vs. "Whichever"

This is a detail that will help you use these words with precision:

  • Whatever = Open-ended. Unlimited possibilities.
    • Example: "Eat whatever you want from the buffet." (There are many options.)
  • Whichever = Limited. A specific, restricted set of options.
    • Example: "Take whichever route is faster—Route A or Route B." (There are only two options.)

Why this is a "Secret Weapon" for TOEFL:

Using whichever correctly demonstrates that you understand nuance. Many students use "whatever" for everything. Using "whichever" when discussing a choice between two or three specific scenarios (e.g., in an Integrated Writing task where you have to compare two reading points or two lecture arguments) shows a high level of grammatical control.

4️⃣ Whomever (meaning: "any person whom" — object form of whoever)

Used when the person is the object of the verb or preposition in the clause. (Note: This is very formal; in casual English, people just use "whoever". But using it can get you a lot of grammar points).

  • Standard: We will hire any person whom the manager recommends.
  • Free: We will hire whomever the manager recommends.
  • Standard: You can give the package to the person to whom it is addressed.
  • Free: You can give the package to whomever it is addressed.

5️⃣ Wherever (meaning: "any place where" / "at whatever place")

This acts as a free relative adverb for place.

  • Standard: I will follow you to any place that you go.
  • Free: I will follow you wherever you go.
  • Standard: In every place where we looked, we found the same pattern.
  • Free: Wherever we looked, we found the same pattern.

6️⃣ Whenever (meaning: "any time when" / "at whatever time")

This acts as a free relative adverb for time.

  • Standard: You can visit me at any time that you want.
  • Free: You can visit me whenever you want.
  • Standard: Every time that the temperature drops, the chemical reaction slows down.
  • Free: Whenever the temperature drops, the chemical reaction slows down.

7️⃣ However (meaning: "in whatever way" or "no matter how")

Used to indicate that the method or the degree does not matter.

  • Standard: In any way that you decide to proceed, I will support you.
  • Free: However you decide to proceed, I will support you.
  • Standard: No matter how you look at this data, the result is the same.
  • Free: However you look at this data, the result is the same.

A Crucial Distinction for "however"

While you can use "however" as a free relative, you should note that it usually functions in two distinct ways. Being able to spot the difference is a huge advantage for your reading comprehension:

1. The "Free Relative" Usage (Adverb of Manner/Degree)

This is the one you identified. It answers how something is done or to what extent.

  • Example: "However you solve the equation, ensure you check your work."
  • (Meaning: In whatever way you solve it…)

2. The "Concession" Usage (The 'Although' meaning)

This is even more common in high-level TOEFL essays. It is often followed by an adjective or adverb.

  • Example: "However difficult the task may be, we must finish it."
  • (Meaning: Even though the task is difficult…)

Why Use Free Relative Clauses?

Using free relative clauses makes your writing:

  • More concise: You eliminate redundant nouns like "the thing" or "any person."
  • Direct: It gets straight to the point, which is useful in both academic analysis and transitions.

Wordy:

I agree with the ideas that the author presented in the second chapter.

Concise:

I agree with what the author presented in the second chapter.

TOEFL Strategy and Placement

Because a free relative clause acts as a noun phrase, it can sit in the two main noun positions of a sentence:

As the Subject of the sentence:

  • What the data show is a clear trend.
  • Whoever designed this study made a critical error.

As the Object of the sentence:

  • We must analyze what caused this shift.
  • The university will admit whoever meets the criteria.

Using a free relative clause as a subject is an excellent way to introduce a key point or summarize a finding in your TOEFL integrated writing task.
